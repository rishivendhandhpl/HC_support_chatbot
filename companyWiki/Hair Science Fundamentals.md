# Hair Science Fundamentals

# **Understanding Hair Structure**

_Verified by Elizabeth & Nicole_
### **The Hair Strand**
A hair strand consists of multiple layers with distinct functions and characteristics. The outer protective layer is called the cuticle, while the inner core contains the cortex and medulla that give hair its strength and color.
The visible color we see in hair comprises only 3% of the entire hair composition. However, to reach and alter that 3% through bleaching or coloring, you must chemically break down approximately 70% of the hair's structure, which causes significant damage to the overall integrity.
### **The Hair Cuticle (Outer Layer)**
The hair cuticle is the protective outer layer of the hair strand, functioning like armor to shield the inner components. This layer consists of overlapping scales that can lay flat or be open, depending on hair porosity. Tightly close and flat = Low Porosity, Slightly loose = Normal/Balanced Porosity & Open cuticles = High Porosity.
The cuticle also acts as a barrier that regulates moisture absorption and retention, which directly impacts how the hair behaves and styles. But note, because of natural variation, hair porosity will differ between batches as some will have low porosity and others will be high, with some balanced cuticles coming into play. Each one will require a different level of care to maintain moisture.

# **Healthy vs. Damaged Cuticles**

### **Healthy Cuticle Characteristics**
A healthy hair cuticle is the outermost, protective layer of a hair strand, consisting of 5–10 layers of overlapping, flat, and smooth keratin scales. This creates a protective barrier that prevents moisture loss, resists tangling, and maintains the hair's natural strength and elasticity.
Healthy cuticles typically layer flat and reflect light uniformly, which creates natural shine and luster in the hair. The tight seal also prevents environmental damage from penetrating into the hair's inner layers, preserving the hair's integrity over time.
### **Cuticle Characteristics (High Porosity Hair)**
High Porosity The cuticle scales are lifted, damaged, or torn, often due to chemical processing, heat, or damage. This allows moisture to enter quickly but escape just as fast, leading to dry, brittle, and frizzy hair.
When cuticles are lifted, the hair becomes highly porous and loses its ability to retain moisture effectively. This results in dry, brittle hair that is prone to frizz and further degradation with each wash or styling session if not properly taken care of.
### **Visual Comparison**
Our microscopy images clearly show the difference between intact and damaged cuticles side by side. In healthy hair, you can see smooth, orderly scales lying flat, while damaged hair shows irregularly lifted scales with visible gaps and roughness.
These visual comparisons help customers understand why damaged hair behaves differently and requires more intensive care. The images also demonstrate why our commitment to preserving the cuticle results in superior quality and performance.

# **What Happens When Hair is Damaged**

### **Increased Porosity**
Hair porosity increases dramatically as moisture can enter and exit too easily. This creates a cycle where the hair absorbs water quickly during washing but loses it just as quickly afterward, leading to chronic dryness.
High porosity from damage means the hair requires constant moisture replenishment and heavy products to maintain hydration. Without proper care, high porosity damaged hair feels rough, looks dull, and tangles easily, regardless of how much product is applied.
### **Tangling and Knotting**
Lifted cuticle scales act like Velcro, catching on adjacent hair strands and creating tangles and knots. The rough texture of damaged cuticles causes strands to grip each other rather than sliding smoothly past one another during movement.
This tangling becomes progressively worse as more cuticles are damaged, creating a cascading effect where tangles cause more friction and more friction causes more damage. Eventually, severely damaged hair can become so tangled that it forms permanent knots requiring cutting to remove.
### **Frizz Development**
Frizz occurs when damaged, lifted cuticles fail to lie flat, causing individual strands to stick out at various angles. The rough surface scatters light rather than reflecting it smoothly, creating a dull, fuzzy appearance instead of sleek shine.
Humidity exacerbates frizz in damaged hair because the lifted cuticles allow excess moisture to penetrate rapidly, causing the hair shaft to swell unevenly. This is why damaged hair becomes significantly frizzier in humid conditions compared to hair with intact cuticles.
### **Loss of Moisture Retention**
Damaged cuticles cannot effectively seal in moisture, creating a constant battle to keep hair hydrated. Even intensive conditioning treatments provide only temporary relief because the compromised cuticle allows moisture to escape rapidly.
This moisture loss makes the hair feel dry and straw-like regardless of how much conditioner or oil is applied. The hair may feel temporarily soft after product application but returns to dryness within hours as the moisture evaporates through the damaged cuticle.
### **Thinner Feel**
Bleaching and chemical processing thin the hair cuticle by breaking down its protective layers. As the cuticle becomes thinner, the entire hair strand feels finer and more fragile, losing the substantial feel of healthy hair.
This thinning effect is particularly noticeable when comparing dark hair to heavily bleached blonde hair from the same source. The blonde hair will consistently feel thinner and more delicate because the bleaching process has literally reduced the thickness of each strand's protective layer.

# **How Hair Damage Occurs**

### **Bleaching and Color Treatments**
Bleaching is one of the most damaging processes for hair because it requires breaking down the cuticle to access the inner pigment. To reach the 3% of the hair that contains color, the bleaching chemicals must destroy approximately 70% of the hair's structural integrity.
Each bleaching session progressively weakens the cuticle, making the hair thinner, more porous, and more vulnerable to breakage. This is why achieving very light blonde shades (Level 11-12) comes with inherent damage that cannot be completely avoided, only minimized through careful technique.
### **Heat Styling Without Protection**
Direct heat from styling tools can literally cook the hair's protein structure, causing permanent damage to the cuticle. When hair is heated above 350°F without protective products, the cuticle scales lift and the internal moisture evaporates rapidly, leaving the hair brittle and weak.
Repeated heat styling without protection creates cumulative damage that worsens over time. Each heating session further degrades the already compromised cuticle, eventually leading to hair that feels like straw and breaks easily even with gentle handling.
### **Chemical Processing**
Perms, relaxers, and other chemical straightening or curling treatments permanently alter the hair's internal bond structure. These processes require breaking and reforming the disulfide bonds that give hair its shape, which inevitably damages the surrounding cuticle and cortex.
The damage from chemical processing is permanent and cannot be reversed through conditioning or treatments. Once these chemical bonds are altered, the hair structure is fundamentally changed and will never return to its original state.
### **Improper Brushing**
Aggressive brushing, especially on wet hair or starting from the roots, can physically rip and tear the cuticle scales. Wet hair is particularly vulnerable because the hydrogen bonds are temporarily broken, making the hair stretch more easily and the cuticle scales more susceptible to lifting.
Using the wrong type of brush or brushing technique creates mechanical damage that accumulates over time. Even healthy hair can become severely damaged through years of improper brushing habits that gradually destroy the cuticle integrity.
### **Environmental Factors**
Sun exposure degrades the hair's protein structure through UV radiation, causing both cuticle damage and color fading. Prolonged sun exposure can make hair feel rough and look dull because the UV rays break down the keratin proteins that form the cuticle.
Chlorine from pools and salt from ocean water both strip moisture and deposit damaging minerals on the hair shaft. These substances penetrate lifted cuticles and cause further damage while creating buildup that makes hair feel coarse and look dull.
### **Product Buildup**
When styling products, oils, and silicones accumulate on the hair shaft, they create a coating that blocks the cuticle. This buildup prevents moisture and beneficial treatments from penetrating the hair while trapping dirt and debris against the cuticle surface.
Over time, product buildup becomes a barrier that makes hair feel heavy, look dull, and resist styling efforts. The accumulated products can also cause the cuticle scales to lift as the hair tries to release the trapped substances, leading to structural damage.

# **The Cuticle Direction Rule**

### **Why "Right Side Up" Matters**
Remy hair must maintain cuticle direction with all scales pointing from root to tip in the same direction. When all cuticles flow in the same direction, the hair strands can slide smoothly past each other without catching or tangling.
This directional uniformity is critical for the hair's longevity and performance because it mimics how hair naturally grows on a human head. Any deviation from this uniform direction creates friction points where strands grip each other rather than gliding smoothly.
### **Cuticle Direction and Tangling**
Hair with mixed cuticle directions experiences severe tangling because scales pointing in opposite directions act like Velcro when they encounter each other. Imagine trying to zip two zippers together when they're facing opposite directions—they catch and bind rather than sliding smoothly.
This is exactly what happens inside a weft or bundle of hair when some strands have cuticles pointing up and others pointing down. The opposing scales interlock and create tangles that become progressively worse with each movement, wash, or styling session.
### **Why Remy Hair Costs More**
Maintaining proper cuticle direction requires extensive hand-sorting and careful processing throughout production. Workers must examine each strand to ensure it flows in the correct direction, removing any strands that are reversed or damaged.
This labor-intensive sorting process is why genuine Remy hair costs significantly more than non-Remy alternatives. The time, skill, and material waste involved in maintaining cuticle integrity make production expensive, but the result is hair that lasts years instead of weeks.

# **Non-Remy Hair Problems**

### **1\. Mixed Cuticle Directions**
Non-Remy hair contains strands with cuticles pointing in multiple directions—some root to tip, others tip to root. This random orientation occurs because manufacturers collect hair from multiple sources without regard for maintaining directional consistency.
When these mixed-direction strands are bundled together, they immediately begin creating friction and tangles. The hair may look acceptable initially when coated with silicone, but once that coating washes out, the tangling problems become severe and unmanageable.
### **2\. Why Companies Strip Cuticles**
Many companies strip the cuticle entirely to avoid the tangling problems caused by mixed directions. If there are no cuticle scales at all, then direction becomes irrelevant because there's nothing to catch on adjacent strands.
After stripping the cuticle, manufacturers coat the hair heavily with silicone to create artificial smoothness and shine. This makes the hair feel decent for a few washes, but once the silicone wears off, the hair becomes rough, tangled, and unusable because it has no natural protective layer remaining.
### **3\. The Silicone Coating Deception**
Silicone coating creates temporary smoothness and shine that masks the underlying quality problems of non-Remy or stripped hair. The coating makes the hair feel soft and look shiny initially, deceiving customers into believing they've purchased high-quality extensions.
However, this silicone washes out within 2-3 shampoos, revealing the true condition of the damaged, stripped hair underneath. Once the coating is gone, the hair becomes rough, tangled, dry, and essentially unusable, requiring replacement far sooner than genuine Remy hair.
### **4\. Rapid Degradation**
Non-Remy hair with stripped cuticles degrades rapidly because it has no natural protection against environmental damage, friction, or moisture loss. Without the cuticle barrier, the inner cortex is exposed directly to damaging elements that break down the hair's protein structure.
Most non-Remy extensions last only 2-3 months at best before becoming too damaged to use. This short lifespan makes them seem less expensive initially, but the frequent replacement requirement makes them far more costly than genuine Remy hair over time.
### **5\. False Advertising**
Many companies falsely advertise their hair as "Remy" when it's actually been stripped and coated with silicone. They rely on customer unfamiliarity with genuine Remy hair characteristics to make these false claims without consequence.
This deceptive practice damages the reputation of the entire industry and creates customer distrust. We combat this by educating customers on how to identify genuine Remy hair through touch, appearance, and performance over time.

# **Hair Weight and Physics**

### **1\. Length Creates Weight**
Longer hair strands contain more mass and therefore weigh more than shorter strands of the same texture. This additional weight is not just theoretical—it creates tangible physical forces that affect how the hair hangs, moves, and maintains its texture.
The weight of long hair pulls downward due to gravity, creating tension along the entire length of each strand. This gravitational pull affects the hair's natural texture, particularly in wavy and curly patterns where the weight can stretch and relax the pattern.
### **2\. Weight Pulls Down Texture**
The weight of long strands literally pulls down and stretches out natural wave and curl patterns. A 16-inch curly strand will show tighter, more defined curls than a 26-inch strand of the same curl pattern because the shorter hair has less weight pulling on it.
This weight effect is particularly noticeable in premium (double drawn) hair where 70% of the strands are long and heavy. The concentration of long, heavy strands in premium hair can make waves appear almost straight, simply due to the downward pull of weight.
### **3\. Why Texture Changes with Length**
Nicole demonstrated this principle using 26-inch curly hair that appeared much looser than shorter curly samples. The curl pattern itself is identical, but the weight of the 26-inch length pulls the curls into a more relaxed, elongated shape that can look almost wavy.
This is not a defect or quality issue—it's simple physics that applies to all hair, natural or extension. Understanding this principle helps set proper customer expectations when ordering longer lengths of textured hair.
### **4\. Premium Finish Weight Effect**
Premium (double drawn) hair contains 70% long strands, creating significantly more weight than layered (single drawn) hair. This concentrated weight pulls textures down more dramatically, which is why premium natural wave appears almost straight at the top while layered natural wave maintains more consistent wave throughout.
In extreme cases, premium extra body wave can look straight from the roots to midshaft, with wave appearing only at the ends where the weight is distributed. This is why we don't offer premium curly hair—the weight would completely eliminate the curl pattern, making it look like wavy hair instead.
### **5\. Considerations for Extension Density**
The total weight of an extension installation depends not just on individual strand length but on how many strands are installed. A client with fine, fragile hair cannot support the same weight as someone with thick, strong hair, regardless of how much volume they desire.
Stylists must carefully calculate the total weight they're adding to a client's head by considering strand length, texture type, finish (premium vs. layered), and number of pieces installed. Overloading a client's natural hair with too much weight causes stress, breakage, and potential hair loss at the attachment points.

# **Impact on Extension Selection**

### **Managing Customer Expectations**
Customers must understand that longer lengths in textured hair will not look exactly like shorter lengths due to the weight effects. A client who loves the tight curl of 14-inch curly hair may be disappointed with 24-inch curly hair that appears much looser and more relaxed.
We educate customers on this principle before they order to prevent dissatisfaction. Showing visual examples of how the same texture appears at different lengths helps customers make informed decisions about what length will achieve their desired look.
### **Why Weight Matters for Custom Orders**
When taking custom orders for textured hair, Nicole considers the weight factor in her recommendations. If a customer wants very defined curls, she may recommend a shorter length to maintain tight curl definition rather than allowing them to order 26 inches that will look wavy due to weight.
This consultative approach prevents customer disappointment and returns. By explaining the physics of hair weight upfront, we help customers order products that will meet their expectations rather than simply taking their order and hoping for the best.
### **Premium vs. Layered Selection**
The weight difference between premium and layered finishes significantly impacts how texture appears. Customers wanting maximum texture definition should choose layered finish where short and long strands create movement without excessive weight pulling down the pattern.
Premium finish is better suited for clients who want length and fullness rather than maximum texture definition. The weight of premium hair creates a sleeker, more substantial look but will relax wave patterns compared to the same texture in layered finish.
